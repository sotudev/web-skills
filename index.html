Liposomal Crocin

Increasing the Anticancerous Properties of Crocus Sativus through the Utilization of Liposomal Nanoparticles

Every year, over 7.5 million people die each year from cancer. While current cancer treatment options such as chemotherapy or radiation therapy are effective in eliminating cancer cells, they often have detrimental side effects on the body. The purpose of this experiment was to create a novel therapeutic cancer treatment with fewer side effects. Research and experimentation was performed on Crocus sativus, specifically its extract Crocin, which proved to have a prominent ability in cancer cell inhibition. However, Crocin proved to have many harmful effects toward healthy cells as well. Also, anti-cancerous properties were exhibited at only very high concentrations. To combat these properties, Crocin was synthesized into liposomal nanoparticles. It was hypothesized that if Liposomal Crocin was applied to cell lines, it would have fewer side effects on healthy cells while maintaining higher inhibition rates on cancerous cell lines. Experimentation was performed on cancerous cell lines HeLa and H1299, and healthy cell lines 2BS and PMBC. Crocin and Liposomal Crocin were applied to all cell lines. Inhibition rates were measured via morphological observation and MTT assay. Results indicated that while both Crocin and Liposomal Crocin inhibited cancer cell growth up to 80% in higher concentrations, Liposomal Crocin was 160 times more efficient. Liposomal Crocin inhibited 50% less healthy cells than Pure Crocin at the ideal rate of 50ug/mL. The hypothesis was supported; Liposomal Crocin was clearly able to inhibit cancer cell proliferation at a significantly higher efficiency and effectiveness while maintaining a better healthy cell survival rate.

Preventing Cancer with Soy

Improving the Chemopreventive Properties of Raloxifene through the Utilization of S-Equol

Every year over 200,000 women are diagnosed with breast cancer in the United States. Breast cancer is the uncontrolled growth and invasion of abnormal cells in mammary tissue which eventually leads to the destruction of healthy cells. Some high-risk women opt to take chemopreventive therapy to reduce carcinogenesis risks. Research and experimentation was performed on raloxifene, a chemopreventive drug. Raloxifene antagonises estrogen receptor alpha in order to prevent transcription of cellular growth-inducing genes. The purpose of this experiment was to improve raloxifene’s efficiency and improve noncancerous cell viability. To improve efficiency, a synergetic treatment combining raloxifene and s-equol, a metabolite produced through soy (a common Chinese medical supplement) preconsumption which blocks estrogen receptor beta, was created. It was hypothesised that there would be a significant increase in apoptotic efficacy against MCF-7 breast cancer cells and that there would be a decrease in 2BS noncancerous cell death. To test this, MTT viability assays, Annexin tests, and morphological analyses were conducted. Against MCF-7 cells, 15 uM of synergy induced apoptosis around 700% more efficiently compared to 80 uM of raloxifene and induced apoptosis 1900% more efficiently than 100 uM of s-equol. Surprisingly, raloxifene killed 15% more 2BS cells than MCF-7 cells. However, the synergy reduced 2BS cell death by 10% compared to raloxifene. The results from this study indicated that the raloxifene/s-equol synergy significantly improved raloxifene’s apoptotic ability against breast cancer cells while mitigating harm against noncancerous cells, and this treatment may warrant future studies in chemoprevention for the future.
